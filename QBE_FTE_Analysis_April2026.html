Georgia QBE FTE Analysis

Prepared April 2026  |  Data Sources: GA DOE QBE FTE & Earnings (FY2023–FY2025), Enrollment by Subgroup Metrics (2023-24, 2024-25)

Purpose: This analysis reviews statewide QBE FTE and earnings trends across three fiscal years and cross-references enrollment headcounts to identify program categories where districts may be under-claiming FTE funding. The gaps identified are signals for deeper investigation with each district — not definitive proof of under-claiming.

1. Data Overview

Seven files were analyzed covering:

  • QBE FTE data: FY2023, FY2024, FY2025 — FTE counts by program category across ~232 Georgia school districts
  • QBE Earnings data: FY2024 and FY2025 — dollar allotments by program category
  • Enrollment by Subgroup: 2023-24 and 2024-25 — headcount enrollment by program, allowing direct enrollment-vs-FTE comparison

2. Statewide Trends

FTE Is Flat; Earnings Are Up Sharply

Total statewide FTE has been essentially flat for three years, while total QBE earnings jumped 7.3% year-over-year, driven by a uniform ~6.5% per-FTE rate increase across all categories — almost certainly a statewide salary schedule adjustment. This means the primary funding lever is program category mix, not raw enrollment growth.

3. Enrollment vs. FTE Gap Analysis

The enrollment data allows us to compare identified/enrolled students in each program to actual FTE claimed. The ratio between these — and how each district compares to the statewide average ratio — reveals where potential under-claiming exists.

5. Summary: Where to Focus First

Based on the data analyzed, the four highest-leverage areas for FTE maximization are:

  1. Gifted service intensity — Many districts have far more identified gifted students than FTE claimed. The gap is often a scheduling or program model issue (pullout 1x/week vs. daily enrichment class). Increasing service hours directly increases FTE.
  2. ESOL documentation and ACCESS administration — Districts with many EL students but low ESOL FTE ratios likely have proficiency level coding issues, late registration, or inadequate program documentation at count time.
  3. SpEd category alignment — Category coding should reflect actual service needs and IEP minutes. A systematic IEP-to-category review at districts with low Cat-III/IV shares often surfaces legitimate reclassification opportunities.
  4. EIP scheduling and documentation — Districts with very low EIP FTE/enrollment ratios should audit whether enrolled students are receiving the minimum instructional minutes required to generate full FTE.
Important note on methodology: All dollar gap estimates in this document are calculated by applying the statewide average FTE-per-student ratio to each district's enrollment count, then multiplying by the FY2025 per-FTE rate. This is an upper-bound signal, not a guaranteed recoverable amount. Actual optimization requires student-level data from each district's SIS to confirm that eligible students are enrolled, properly coded, and receiving services of sufficient duration at the time of the FTE count.

Analysis prepared using GA DOE public data. FTE rates derived from QBE Earnings ÷ QBE FTE by category, FY2025.
